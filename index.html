UNDISCOVERED INTERNET

There are parts of the internet that are not widely known or accessible to the general public. These include private networks, password-protected websites, and other content that is not intended to be publicly available. For example, some companies or organizations may have private networks that are only accessible to employees or authorized individuals. There are also closed communities and forums that require an invitation or referral to join. Additionally, there are parts of the internet that are intentionally hidden, such as the dark web, which can only be accessed through special software and is known for being a hub for criminal activity. It's important to be cautious when exploring these parts of the internet and to take steps to protect your privacy and security.

LAYERS OF THE WEB
SURFACE WEB

The surface web refers to the portion of the World Wide Web that is indexed and accessible through standard search engines like Google, Bing, Yahoo, and others. This includes websites that are publicly available and can be accessed through standard web browsers using the http or https protocol. In other words, the surface web is the part of the internet that most people are familiar with, and includes websites like social media platforms, online news outlets, e-commerce sites, and other web pages that are easily accessible to anyone with an internet connection. It's important to note that the surface web represents only a small fraction of the internet as a whole. The majority of the internet is made up of the deep web and the dark web, which are not indexed by search engines and are only accessible through specialized software or authentication protocols.

DEEP WEB

The deep web is the part of the internet that is not indexed by search engines and cannot be accessed through regular web browsers. This includes private networks, password-protected websites, and other content that is not intended to be publicly available. While some of the content on the deep web is harmless, there is also a lot of illegal and dangerous content, such as black markets, hacking forums, and other criminal activity. It's important to be cautious when exploring the deep web and to take steps to protect your privacy and security.

HOW TO ACCESS

How to access the deep web

Although the deep web's information isn't indexed by regular search engines, it can often still be accessed. Accessing content on the deep web is relatively safe, and most internet users do it all the time. Logging into Gmail or LinkedIn, or signing in to the Wall Street Journal are just a few examples of accessing data on a deep web site. User accounts on the deep web contain a lot of personal information that criminals might value -- that's why access to much of the deep web is restricted. The deep web, including the dark web, will never come to users. Spam and phishing attacks may originate from a dark web marketplace, but a user has to download something infected from that marketplace to release malware. An attack would not originate from the dark web site itself. The dark web is intentionally hidden and requires specific technologies -- like the Tor browser and the Invisible Internet Project (I2P) network -- to gain access. Both tools have legitimate uses. Tor will protect your IP when visiting websites and I2P is a proxy network that can help journalists reporting from dangerous territories.
Users cannot access a dark web marketplace without the Tor browser. And while it's based on the Mozilla Firefox browser, Tor is not as well maintained and has page rendering issues. There is one legitimate reason to peruse the dark web. With all the discussions related to hacking and exploit trading on the dark web, it is a great place to see where yet-unknown vulnerabilities are being discussed. By monitoring the dark web, users might gain the advantage of knowing where exploits are before they become widespread threats.

How to access the dark web

Accessing the dark web requires the use of an anonymizing browser called Tor. The Tor browser routes your web page requests through a series of proxy servers operated by thousands of volunteers around the globe, rendering your IP address unidentifiable and untraceable. Tor works like magic, but the result is an experience that’s like the dark web itself: unpredictable, unreliable and maddeningly slow.
